\chapter{Voting Schemes}
\label{chap:votingschemes}
Voting schemes are used to conduct elections. 
Here, several voters submit ballots which are then aggregated by a voting authority. 
After that, the voting authority publishes the final tally of the election.

We will now formalize this approach and iteratively extend it to fulfill several security considerations.

To motivate the different steps, we introduce a simple example: Electing a new president of the Computer Science Club at a university.
The club has the five members Alice (A), Bob (B), Charlie (C), Derpy (D) and Eve (E). 
Three of these members are running for the position, namely Alice, Bob and Eve.
In the election, each of the five clubs' members can vote for one candidate. 
It is possible to abstain from the vote.
% The votes will be counted by the voting authority ($VA$) Fred (F) who does not participate in the election.
In the end, the candidate receiving the most votes overall wins the election and becomes the next president.

In a first attempt at implementing this election, each of the members writes their chosen candidate on a piece of paper. 
The ballots are then collected by Fred who counts how often each candidate was choosen and posts the result to a public bulletin board ($PBB$).
Fred does not participate in the election.

This first version of the voting scheme is illustrated in \cref{fig:ExVS1}.

\begin{figure}[h]

  \caption{First naive implementation of the example voting scheme}

One obvious problem with this approach is the lack of consistency of the ballots submitted by the members.
For example, it is not clear what to do if a voter misspells the name of a candidate on their ballot. 
We can combat this problem by standardizing the ballot format.

\section{Ballot Format}
In every voting scheme, the ballots submitted by the voters need to be chosen from specific choice space $C$. 
In our example, every voter now has to submit a ballot from $C:=\{(1,0,0), (0,1,0), (0,0,1), (0,0,0)\}$. 
Here a ballot $(a, b, e)$ signifies that the member gave $a$ votes to Alice, $b$ votes to Bob and $e$ Votes to Eve.

Another benefit of standardized ballots is that the votes can now be counted automatically.
The new voting authority $VA$ can aggregate the ballots by adding them component-wise via a function $tally$.
Then, they can publish the result of this computation.
The winner of the election can be determined by anyone based on this aggregated ballot.
The new version of our example voting scheme is illustrated in \cref{fig:ExVS2}.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.5\textwidth]{graphics/votingSchemes/EXVotingScheme_2.pdf}
  \caption{The example voting scheme with standardized ballot chosen from choice space $C$ and an automatic voting authority aggregating the ballots.}
  \label{fig:ExVS2}
\end{figure}

With this new voting system, abstention by not casting a ballot is no longer possible. 
Instead, no preference for any candidate is represented by casting a ballot $(0, 0, 0)$.
We chose to model abstention explicitly since this has security benefits compared to silent abstention where voters don't cast a ballot at all.
For instance, it is more difficult for misbehaving parties to insert ballots into an election if every voter already cast a ballot.
In such a case $VA$ can detect that that the election was influenced even if they might not know which ballots are fraudulent.
However, the downside of explicit abstantion is aloss of convenience for the user as they always have to cast a ballot now.

In the current version of the voting scheme, votes are transmitted to the voting authority in plain.
This means that any party eavedropping this connection can see who the individual members voted for.
To prevent this, we need to add encryption.

\section{Confidentiality of Ballots}
To ensure that ballots are secret and nobody but the voters themself know what they voted for, ballots are encrypted component-wise by an encryption function $Enc$ before being submitted.
We use an asymmetric encryption scheme for the encryption and decryption process as this allows users to encrypt ballots using a public key $pk$ and without having to exchange a private key $sk$ beforehand.
Moreover, it is important to choose an additively homomorphic encryption scheme. 
With additive homomorphism, the voting authority can still aggregate the individual entries at corresponding positions of ballots by performing some computation $agg$ on the components of the encrypted ballots.
The result of the election can then be obtained by decrypting the comonents of the output of $agg$ using the corresponding decryption function $Dec$ of the encryption scheme.
Since the encrypted ballots don't reveal any information about the plain ballots, it doesn't matter who obtains them. 
Therefore, the voters can submit their ballots to the public bulletin board and the voting authority can take them from there.
This version of the example voting scheme is illustrated in \cref{fig:ExVS3}.

The voting authority needs to have access to the private key of the used encrytion scheme to be able to decrypt the final tally of the election.
However, this means that a misbehaving voting authority can also decrypt individual votes. 
One approach to combat this problem is Threshhold encryption as presented by Bernhard and Warinschi in section 4.1 of \cite{aldini_cryptographic_2014}.

\section{Integrity of Ballots and Election Result}
To ensure that no malicous voter can submit ballots on behalf of other voters and that only $VA$ can publish the final election result, we employ digital signatures.
Here, every voter $V$ and $VA$ have a public-private key pair $(pk_V, sk_V)$ and $(pk_{VA}, sk_{VA})$ that is different from the key pair used for encryption.
We asssume that it is public knowledge which public key belongs to which voter and which public key belongs to $VA$.
This knowledgge can be obtained using a \gls{pki} based on a hierarchy of certificate authorities mapping public keys to entities as presented in section 13.6 of \cite{katz_introduction_2021}.
% $s_V=Sig(b'_V, sk_v)$
% $Ver(b'_V, s_V, pk_V)$ returns $valid$

Then, every time a party $P$ publishes a message $m$ to $PBB$, they also submit a signature $s=Sign(m, sk)$ on $m$ signed with their private key $sk$.
Since the public keys are public information, everybody can verify the signature $s$ on $m$ to check that it was actually $P$ who submitted the message by running the verification algorithm $Vrfy(m, s, pk)$.

For instance, each voter submits a signature on their encrypted ballot $b'$ alongside the ballot itself and the voting authority additionally submits a signature on the final tally of the election $b_{total}$.
Using the verification algorithm, the voting authority can check the signature on an encrypted ballot $b'$.
Only if the verification is successful will the voting authority consider $b'$ in the aggregation.
Similarly, any voter can check the final result of the election by verifying the signature of the voting authority on $b_{total}$.

% $s_{VA}=Sig(b_{total}, sk_{VA})$
% $Ver(b_{total}, s_{VA}, pk_{VA})$
% Similarly, $VA$ publishes a signature $s_{VA}$ on the decrypted tally of the election $b_{total}$ together with this total.
% If some party wants to check the election result they can onbtain $s_{VA}$ and $b_{total}$ from $PBB$ and verify $b_{total}$ via $Ver$.
% The modified version of our election scheme is illustrated in \cref{fig:ExVS4}.

Since integrity checks are not the focus of this thesis, we abstract away from the details and do not formalize the use of digital signatures in our voting schemes.
However, for the rest of the thesis, keep in mind that any message published to $PBB$ needs to be signed by the party publishing it and that any information retrieved from $PBB$ comes with a signature that needs to be checked before the information is used.

Even after ensuring the integrity of submitted ballots and ensuring that only $VA$ can publish the final election result, two problems that are directly connected remain.
In the current version of the voting scheme, voters can submit ballots that are not in the choice space to gain an unfair advantage. 
In our example Eve could submit a vote $(0,0,100)$ to win the election as shown in \cref{fig:ExVS3Prob2}.

Therefore, we need mechanisms to ensure the following:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The encrypted ballot submitted by the voter actually belongs to the plain ballot they chose.
  \item The plain ballot chosen by the voter is actually part of the choice space.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Ensuring Correct Ballot Format and Correct Encryption of Ballots}
To make sure that voters can only choose ballots from the choice space and then also encrypt exactly these ballots, we employ \glspl{zkp}. 

The goal of \glspl{zkp} is to convince some party of the truth of a statement without revealing any confidential information to them.
In our example, this would mean convincing the general public that the ballot a voter chose is part of the choice space and belongs to the encrypted ballot they submitted.
 
For now we will look at \glspl{zkp} as Black-Box-Protocols $ZKP=(Setup, Prove, Verify)$. 
Here, the proving algorithm $Prove$ takes some public inputs $I$ (also called instance) and some private inputs $W$ (also called witness) and produces a \gls{zkp} $\pi$.
$\pi$ proves that the party who executed $Prove$ knows a corresponding witness $W$ for the instance $I$, even though only the instance is published.
Anyone can verify the proof $\pi$ by running the verifying algorithm $Verify$ on $\pi$ and the instance $I$.

In a voting scheme we use the \gls{zkp} $(Setup_C, Prove_C, Verify_C)$, where each voter $V$ runs the proving algorithm $Prove_C$ to obtain a \gls{zkp} $\pi_V$.
Here, the witness is the plain ballot $b_V$ they chose and the instance is the corresponding encrypted ballot $b'_V$.\footnote{
  \label{footnote:encryptedBallotInCircuit}
  Note that it is also possible to compute the encrypted ballot $b'_V$ as part of the \gls{zkp} rather than providing it as an input for it.
  However, both \gls{zkp} variants are equally efficient.
  Since we can compute the encryption of a ballot efficiently before providing it as an input to the \gls{zkp} using SageMath \cite{matthias_koppe_sagemath_2025}, we choose this variant for ease of explanation.
}
$\pi$ then proves that $b_V\in C$ and $Enc(b_V)=b'_V$.
Finally, the member publishes the encrypted ballot $b'_V$ along with the \gls{zkp} $\pi_V$ to the PBB.

Before aggregating the ballots, the voting authority runs $Verify_C$ for each $(\pi_V, (b'_V, C))$. 
The voting authority only accepts ballots with a valid \gls{zkp} $\pi_V$. 
This prevents a malicous voter like $E$ from submitting ballots that are not part of the choice space $C$.
This version of our example voting scheme is illustrated in \cref{fig:ExVS4}.

  \includegraphics[width=.8\textwidth]{graphics/votingSchemes/EXVotingScheme_4.pdf}
  \caption{The example voting scheme as in \cref{fig:ExVS3}. Additionally voters use \glspl{zkp} to show that the ballots they chose are in the choice space $C$ and belong to the submitted encrypted ballots.}
  \label{fig:ExVS4}
\end{figure}

With this, we have reached the voting scheme we will use throughout the rest of this thesis.

\section{Defining Voting Schemes}

We can now define what a voting scheme is formally. 
Here, we use asymmetric encryption schemes as presented in \cite{katz_introduction_2021}.
The voting scheme is based on the approach presented in \cite{cortier_fifty_2020}.
However, to simplify things a little, we assume that the voting authority and the public bulletin board are both honest and that the public bulletin board is available.
\footnote{\label{footnote:honestAvailablePBB}
  An \glqq honest\grqq\ $PBB$ does not discard any information published and answers queries truthfully.
  It is possible to model voting systems with misbehaving bulletin boards. 
  One such approach is described in \cite{cortier_fifty_2020}.
  To simplify the construction we will only consider an honest public bulletin board.
  \glqq Available\grqq\ means that all voters and the voting authority can publish entries to $PBB$ and query $PBB$ at any point. 
  Neither information to publish nor answers from $PBB$ can be blocked.
}

\begin{definition}[Choice space]
  \label{def:choiceSpace}
    A choice space $C$ is a set of allowed ballots. 
    Ballots are always either vectors or matrices of field elements.

    Formally, Let $(\mathbb{F}, +, \cdot)$ be a sufficiently large finite field.\footnote{\label{footnote:choiceSpaceSize}
      Intuitively, \glqq Sufficiently large\grqq\ means that the aggregated tally of plain ballots does not exceed the characteristic $char_\mathbb{F}$ of the field $\mathbb{F}$.
      More formally: 
      Consider an election with $n$ voters and a choice space $C$ over $\mathbb{F}$.
      % Let $g$ be a generator of $(\mathbb{F}\setminus\{0\},\cdot)$.
      Let $b\in C$ be a ballot. 
      Let $v_{max}$ be the largest allowed value for an individual entry in $b$.
      %Then, we can represent $v$ as $g^{m}$ for some $m\in\{0,\dots, |\mathbb{F}|-1\}$.
      Then, the field $\mathbb{F}$ has a sufficient size if $n\cdot v_{max} < char_\mathbb{F}$.
    }
    Then, a choice space $C$ over $\mathbb{F}$ fulfills: 
    \begin{itemize}
      \item $C\neq \emptyset$
      \item $\exists i\in\mathbb{N}:C\subseteq\mathbb{F}^{i}\cup \quad\lor\quad \exists i,j\in\mathbb{N}: C\subseteq\mathbb{F}^{i\times j}$.
    \end{itemize}
    
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Public Bulletin Board]
  \label{def:pbb}
  A public bulletin board $PBB$ is a dictionary of public information.
  Anyone with access to this board can publish new information or obtain existing information.
  Formally, $PBB=(publish, obtain)$. 
  Here, $publish(id, x)$ stores a the information $x$ under the identifier $id$. 
  $obtain(id)$ will return $x$ if $publish(id, x)$ was executed by any party sometime before and $\bot$ otherwise.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Relation \cite{least_authority_moonmath_2024}]
  \label{def:relation}
  Let $\mathcal{I}$ be a set of private instances, let $\mathcal{W}$ be a set of public witnesses.
  Then, a relation $R$ between instances and witnesses is defined as follows:
  \begin{align*}
    R:\mathcal{I}\times\mathcal{W}\to\{0,1\},\:R(I, W) = \begin{cases}
      1, \quad\text{if } I \text{ and } W \text{ are related}\\
      0, \quad\text{otherwise.}
    \end{cases}
  \end{align*}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Zero Knowledge Proof System for Relation $R$ \cite{groth_size_2016}]
  \label{def:zpsForR}
  Let $R$ be a binary polynomial time decidable relation \cite{least_authority_moonmath_2024} relating instances $I\in\mathcal{I}$ and witnesses $W\in\mathcal{W}$.
  A zero knowledge proof system $ZPS(R)$ for the relation $R$ consists of three algorithms: 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item $Setup()$: This algorithm generates some auxiliary public information $CRS$ ("common reference string") that is needed to coduct and verify proofs for the relation $R$.
    \item $Prove(CRS, I, W)$: This algorithm uses the $CRS$ to generate a proof $\pi$, stating that the public instance $I\in\mathcal{I}$ and the private witness $W\in\mathcal{W}$ are related, i.e., $R(I, W)=1$.
    Here, $\pi$ proves that the party executing $Prove(CRS, I, W)$ knows the private witness $W$ without revealing any information about $W$.
    The party executing $Prove$ is also called \glqq Prover\grqq.
    \item $Verify(CRS, I, \pi)$: This algorithm uses the $CRS$ to verify a proof $\pi$. This algorithm will return $valid$ or $invalid$. 
    If $Verify(CRS, I, \pi)$ returns $valid$, this means that the party who created $\pi$ knows a private witness $W$ corresponding to $I$ such that $R(I, W)=1$.
    The party executing $Verify$ is also called \glqq Verifier\grqq.
  \end{itemize}
  Formally, 
  \begin{align*}
    ZPS(R)=(Setup, Prove, Verify)\text{.}
  \end{align*}
  The zero-knowledge property of the proof system states the following: If a party runs $Verify(CRS, I, \pi)$ successfully, they don't learn anything about the witness $W$ corresponding to $I$ other than that the party who created $\pi=Prove(CRS, I, W)$ knows $W$.
  Furthermore, any zero knowledge proof system is required to be both sound and complete \cite{least_authority_moonmath_2024}:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Soundness: If an instance $I$ and a witness $W$ are related, then 
    \begin{align*}
      Verify(CRS, I, Prove(CRS, I, W))=valid\text{.}
    \end{align*}
    \item Completeness: If an instance $I$ and a witness $W$ are not related, then 
    \begin{align*}
      Verify(CRS, I, Prove(CRS, I, W))=invalid
    \end{align*} 
    except with some small probability.
  \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Voting Scheme]
  \label{def:votingScheme}
  A voting scheme with $n$ voters is a seven-tuple of the form 
  \begin{align*}
    VS=(C, \mathcal{S}, ZPS(R_C), PBB, Setup, \{V_0, \dots, V_{n-1}\}, VA)\text{.}
  \end{align*}
  Here, we use the following:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item $C$ is a choice space.
    \item $\mathcal{S}=(Gen, Enc, Dec)$ is a homomorphic encryption scheme as presented by Katz and Lindell in definitions 12.1 and 15.14 of \cite{katz_introduction_2021}.
    We use $Enc^{aux}(x, pk)$\footnote{\label{footnote:compwiseEncDec}
      To simplify notation, we also denote the componentwise encryption of a ballot $b\in C$ under auxiliary information $aux$ by $Enc^{aux}(b)$ and the componentwise decryption of an encrypted tally $b'_{total}$ by $Dec(b'_{total}, sk)$.
      For instance, for $C=\{0,\dots, m-1\}^l$, we encrypt a ballot $b=(b_0,\dots, b_{l-1})$ under $aux=(aux_0,\dots, aux_{l-1})$ by $Enc^{aux}(b):=(Enc^{aux_0}(b_0),\dots, Enc^{aux_{l-1}}(b_{l-1}))$.
    } to denote the encryption of a plaintext $x$ under auxiliary information $aux$ with public key $pk$. 
    Since $Enc$ needs to be probabilistic to achieve security, $aux$ models the randomness used for the encryption of $x$.
    Similarly, we use $Dec(y, sk)$\cref{footnote:compwiseEncDec} to denote the deterministic decryption of a ciphertext $y$ with the secret key $sk$.
    % For instance, $aux$ can include the randomness used in the non-deterministic encryption.
    % Here, $C$ must be a subset of the message space $\mathcal{M}_{pk}$ for any public key $pk$ generated by $Gen$.
    
    % \item $\mathcal{M}=(Gen, Sign, Vrfy)$ is a digital signature scheme as defined by Katz and Lindell in section 13 of \cite{katz_introduction_2021}.
    % We use $Sign(m, sk)$ to denote the signature of a message $m$ under the secret key $sk$.
    % Similarly, we denote the verification of the signature $s$ for the message $m$ with the public key $pk$ by $Vrfy(m, s, pk)$.
    \item $R_C$ is a relation indicating ballot validity.
    To do so, $R_C$ relates public instances $(pk, b')$ and private witnesses $(aux, b)$.
    Here, $R_C((pk, b'), (aux, b))=1$ iff $b\in C$, $pk$ is a public key of $\mathcal{S}$, and $\mathcal{S}.Enc^{aux}(b, pk)=b'$. 
    Otherwise, $R_C$ maps to $0$.
    \item $ZPS(R_C)$ is a zero-knowledge proof system used to prove ballot validity. 
    \glqq Ballot validity\grqq\ as defined by the relation $R_C$ above.
    %Here, $Prove(CRS, (pk, b'), (aux, b))$ uses a plain ballot $b$ and the auxiliary information from the encryption of $b$ as the private witness as well as a public key $pk$ and the encrypted ballot $b'$ as an instance.
    %The proof $\pi$ created by $Prove(CRS, (pk, b'), (aux, b))$ then shows that the ballot $b$ a voter chose is in $C$ and that $Enc^{aux}(b, pk)=b'$.
    \footnote{\label{footnote:zkpKnowVsExists}

      It is possible to combat replay attacks by rejecting duplicated ballots \cite{mestel_how_2022} however, this is beyond the scope of our work.
      %\todo{Is Malleability of Groth16 a problem if all instances are involved in the constraints?}

      %In the case of voting schemes, this means that a malicous voter might be able to submit an encrypted ballot without knowing the corresponding plain ballot.
      %To prevent this issue, we can modify Groth16 slightly by extending the relation $R_C$ to include an additional constraint on the instance that is always fulfilled.

    }
    %Consequently, $Verify(CRS, (C, pk, b'), \pi)$ uses the public key and the encrypted ballot to verify the proof $\pi$.
    \item $PBB$ is an honest and available public bulletin board.\cref{footnote:honestAvailablePBB}
    \item $Setup$ is used at the start of the election to setup $ZPS$, the voters and the voting authority and publish all information to $PBB$.
    Here, the $Setup$ procedure is defined by \cref{alg:setupVotingScheme}.
    \item $V_0,\dots, V_{n-1}$ are voters. 
    Each Voter $V$ is a three-tuple of the form $V=(PBB, Vote, id_V)$. $Vote$ is defined by \cref{alg:vote}.
    \item $VA$ is the voting authority tallying the valid votes submitted to the $PBB$.
    Here, $VA$ is a five-tuple of the form $VA=(PBB, sk, SetupEnc, agg, Tally)$. 
    $SetupEnc$ is called by the $Setup$ procedure of the voting system with the encryption scheme $\mathcal{S}$ and used to generate the public private key pair.
    Here, the $SetupEnc(\mathcal{S})$ proedure is defined in \cref{alg:setupEncryption}.

  \label{alg:setupVotingScheme}
  \begin{algorithmic}
  \Procedure{$Setup$}{}
    \State $CRS\probgets ZPS.Setup()$
    \State $pk\probgets VA.SetupEnc(\mathcal{S})$
    \State $PBB.publish(setup, (C, ZPS, CRS, \mathcal{S}, pk, n))$
  \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{Algorithmus}

\begin{Algorithmus}[h] %Use the environment only if you want to place the algorithm similar to graphics from TeX
  \caption{Setup of the encryption in a $VS=(C, \mathcal{S}, ZPS, PBB, Setup, \{V_0, \dots, V_{n-1}\}, VA)$ executed by $VA=(PBB, sk, SetupEnc, agg, Tally)$.}
  \label{alg:setupEncryption}
  \begin{algorithmic}
  \Procedure{$SetupEnc$}{$\mathcal{S}$}
    \State $(pk, sk)\probgets \mathcal{S}.Gen(1^\eta)$
    \State \Return $pk$
  \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{Algorithmus}

\begin{Algorithmus}[h] %Use the environment only if you want to place the algorithm similar to graphics from TeX
  \caption{Voting by a voter $V=(PBB, vote, id_V)$.}
  \label{alg:vote}
  \begin{algorithmic}
  \Procedure{$Vote$}{}
    \State $(C, ZPS, CRS, \mathcal{S}, pk, n)\gets PBB.obtain(setup)$
    \State Choose ballot $b\in C$.
    \State Choose auxiliary information $aux$ needed for encryption.
    \State $b'\probgets \mathcal{S}.Enc^{aux}(b, pk)$
    \State $\pi\gets ZPS.Prove(CRS, (pk, b'), (aux, b))$
    \State $PBB.publish(id_V, (b', \pi))$
  \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{Algorithmus}

\begin{Algorithmus}[h]
  \caption{Tallying by a voting authority $VA=(PBB, sk, Setup, agg, Tally)$.}
  \label{alg:tally}
  \begin{algorithmic}
  \Procedure{$Tally$}{}
    \State $(C, ZPS, CRS, \mathcal{S}, pk, n)\gets PBB.obtain(setup)$
    \State $toAgg \gets []$
    \ForAll{$i \in \{0,\dots, n-1\}$}
      \State $pb\gets PBB.obtain(id_{V_i})$ 
      %\cite{footnote:vaRegisterVoters}
      \If{$pb \neq \bot$}
        \State $(b'_i, \pi_i) := pb$
        \If{$ZPS.Verify(CRS, (pk, b'_i), \pi_i) = valid$}
          \State $toAgg.add(b'_i)$
        \EndIf
      \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State $b'_{total}\gets agg(toAgg)$
    \State $b_{total}\gets \mathcal{S}.Dec(b'_{total}, sk)$
    \State $PBB.publish(tally, b_{total})$
  \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{Algorithmus}
