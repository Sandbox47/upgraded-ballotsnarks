\chapter{Introduction}

Homomorphic aggregation is a widely used method in secure electronic voting (e-voting) schemes. 
In these schemes, a ballot submitted by a voter is typically represented as a vector or a matrix.
Here, each entry represents a choice made by the voter, for instance whether a specific candidate receives a vote or not.
Then, every entry is encrypted individually using an additively homomorphic encryption scheme like \gls{eeg}, resulting in a vector or matrix of ciphertexts. 
These encrypted ballots are then aggregated by a voting authority and decrypted to obtain the final tally.

To ensure the integrity of the tally, it is important that each encrypted ballot encodes a valid vote. 
Otherwise, a malicious voter could manipulate the tally by submitting malicously chosen votes. 
To prevent this, voters provide a zero knowledge proof (ZKP) of ballot validity.
This proof shows that their encrypted vote lies within the set of allowed choices without revealing the actual vote.

Traditionally, ZKPs for ballot validity are crafted specifically for certain election types, ballot formats, and encryption schemes. 
Even though these specialized ZKPs are efficient, they usually can not be easily applied to new voting methods or ballot structures. 
For more complex election types, such as Borda elections or Condorcet methods, specialized ZKPs become increasingly difficult to design and often impose significant efficiency trade-offs.

As an alternative, we can use \glspl{gpzkp} one form of which are zk-\glspl{snark}.
\glspl{gpzkp} can be employed to prove arbitrary statements as long as they can be expressed through as the correct execution of an arithmetic circuit. 
Therefore, we can construct proofs of ballot validity by implementing appropriate arithmetic circuits.
This approach is more flexible than specialized \glspl{zkp} and allows us to construct new voting schemes for novel ballot formats easily.

The practicality of \gls{gpzkp} for ballots encrypted with \gls{eeg} has been explored by Huber et. al. \cite{huber_zk-snarks_2025}.
In their work Huber et. al. implemented circuits showing ballot validity for the different election types Single-Vote, Multi-Vote and the variations Line-Vote and \gls{mwr}, the Borda election types Pointlist-Borda and \gls{bts}, Condorcet methods and Majority-Judgment in libsnark.
The benchmark results obtained by Huber et. al. demonstrate that Groth16 based \glspl{zkp} can remain efficient even for complex choice spaces such as \gls{bts} or Condorcet methods.
