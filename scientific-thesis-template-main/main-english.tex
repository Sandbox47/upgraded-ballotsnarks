\section*{Abstract}

In today's world, the necessity of secure e-voting schemes is undisputed. 
In many cases, Zero Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs) are used to allow users to prove the validity of their (encrypted) votes without revealing them. 
One class of ZKPs that can be used to prove (essentially) arbitrary valid statements are General Purpose ZKPs (GPZKPs) with Zero-Knowledge Succinct Non-interactive Arguments of Knowledge (zk-SNARKs) being one form often used in practice for efficiency reasons.
% Here, for efficiency reasons, Zero-Knowledge Succinct Non-interactive Arguments of Knowledge (zk-SNARKs) are often used in practice.

The feasability of GPZKPs for showing validity of ballots that are component-wise encrypted using Exponential ElGamal (EEG) ciphertexts in voting systems with arbitrary election types and ballot formats using Groth16-SNARKs \cite{groth_size_2016} has been shown by Huber et al. \cite{huber_zk-snarks_2025,huber_kryvos_2022}.
To demonstrate the versatility of GPZKPs, the authors implemented ballot validity relations for the different election types Single-Vote, Multi-Vote, Line-Vote, Multi-Vote with Rules, the ranked election types Pointlist-Borda and Borda Tournament Style, and Condorcet methods in libsnark and benchmarked their performance.

We extend on this research by implementing ballot validity relations for the same circuits in Circom and generating the \glspl{zkp} using snarkjs.
First, we follow the same approach as Huber et. al. for our implementation using EEG encryption instantiated with Montgomery curves for the encryption of individual ballot entries.

Then, as our main contribution, we introduce a novel variant of EEG encrpytion: Precomputed Powers EEG (PPEEG). 
By providing some precomputed powers of group elements to the encryption circuit, we reduce the computations needed for ZKPs of ballot validity.
Additionally, we instantiate PPEEG encryption with Twisted Edwards curves rather than Montgomery curves reducing the computational effort further.

Furthermore, we improve the performance of the election type specific computations in the ballot validity proofs for several election types, most notably for Condorcet methods.
However, these improvements are negligible compared to the reduction of computational effort in the encryption part in most cases.

Compared to the results by Huber et. al, we reduce the computational effort for the ballot validity proofs accross all election types by $35\%$ to $82\%$ depending on the metric.
Moreover, we can compute ballot validity proofs for ballots with up to $1000$ votes on a standard PC which is sufficient for almost all real-world applications of any of the covered election types.
